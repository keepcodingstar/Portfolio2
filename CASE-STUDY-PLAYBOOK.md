# The Case Study Playbook

*A practical, cited framework for writing product-design case studies that land a **mid-level** role. Synthesized from hiring-side and practitioner sources (IxDF, NN/g, Bestfolios, DesignerUp, UX Playbook, Design Bootcamp), adversarially fact-checked. Confidence and dissent noted throughout.*

---

## The one-sentence thesis

> A great case study is a **connected narrative with the bottom line up front** — not a clinical "problem → research → wireframes → final" report. It proves *how you think*, quantifies *what changed*, and is built to be understood by a skeptical reviewer in about **5 minutes of skimming**.

---

## 0. The reviewer reality (design for this)

These are conventions/benchmarks from the sources, not measured laws — but they're consistent enough to design against:

- A hiring manager may spend **~5 minutes on your *entire* portfolio**, and skim a single case study in roughly **60–90 seconds** before deciding to read on. *(IxDF; corroborated across UX Playbook, User Interviews.)*
- They primarily look at **case studies**, and value **how you arrived at the solution over the polished final outcome**. *(IxDF.)*
- **Articulation can beat visual polish.** Hiring manager Kiley Rundle: designers with *stunning* portfolios who couldn't explain their work lost to designers with *less striking* portfolios who could clearly narrate the problems they tackled. *(Design Bootcamp.)*

**Implication:** every case study must survive a 90-second skim *and* reward a 5-minute read. Front-load the payload; make the depth scannable.

## 1. Anatomy & structure

### Reject the rigid template — but don't abandon structure

The generic linear template (intro → problem → research → wireframes → process → solution → impact) "**puts readers to sleep**" because the sections read as disconnected, clinical facts. The fix is to **rethink** structure into a story arc, not to drop structure. *(UX Playbook, IxDF, Design Bootcamp — vote 3-0.)*

Recommended arc:

```
CONTEXT  →  STORY  →  RESULTS
(cover,      (research INSIGHTS →    (impact/metrics,
 summary,     3 key decisions →       lessons learned)
 role)        design → validation)
```

Each section should land as a **consequence of the one before it** (cause-and-effect), not a checklist item.

### The two mandatory sections

Across every project, only two sections are truly non-negotiable: **the hook** and the **outcomes / lessons learned**. Research, sketches/wireframing, and prototyping are *flexible* — emphasize them selectively per project rather than always including the full set. *(IxDF — vote 3-0.)*

### A sound baseline scaffold (use it as a skeleton, not a script)

> problem → goals → role/process → research/interviews → ideation/wireframing → solution/MVP → outcomes → reflection

This is a *sound scaffold* (vote 2-1) — but what elevates the best studies is **reasoning, decisions, and tradeoffs woven through it**. Rote, mechanical execution of the template is the thing critics call the "case-study factory" that "numbs critical thinking." Use the bones; don't let them show.

> ⚠️ **Contested:** A rigid **six-part** structure (Context/Challenge/Process/Decisions/Outcome/Reflection) and a fixed **"3 success metrics"** rule were both **refuted** (votes 0-3 and 1-2). Treat any single rigid skeleton skeptically — consensus favors a **curated narrative** over a fixed sequence.

## 2. The hook (first screen)

- **Title formula: subject + challenge + impact.** The *challenge* is the curiosity hook. *(Design Bootcamp — 3-0.)*
  - Weak: "Checkout Redesign"
  - Strong: "Rebuilding checkout to recover a 2.68% conversion gap"
- **Open with BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front):** state the **problem, the solution, and the impact (with metrics)** *before* background, so a skimmer grasps and trusts the work in ~90 seconds. *(Design Bootcamp, IxDF — 3-0.)*
  - Morgane Peng (Head of Design, Societe Generale CIB): *"I'd rather see the results first before diving into the case study."*
  - Real examples: **Elise Fu**'s Komeeda study front-loads **+676% DAU / +223% transactions** before "Background"; **Simon Pan**'s Uber study leads with the business cost (**~$1M/week wasted**); **Madeline Wukusick** opens with results.
- The first screen should **spark interest, not bury the reader in setup.**

---

## 3. Demonstrating rigor without boring anyone

- **Lead with research *insights*, not methodology.** Never use research methods as section titles. *(Design Bootcamp, IxDF — 3-0.)*
  - Reporting: *"I ran 5 user interviews and a survey."*
  - Storytelling: *"Users abandoned at payment because they didn't trust the total — here's what I heard."* (Mention the method in passing; subordinate it to the insight it produced.)
- Listing "research" then jumping to "wireframes" with no bridge makes it look like you **ticked research off a checklist.** Show the causal thread: *insight → decision → design.*
- **Distill the problem into ~3 key decisions / tradeoffs.** *(UX Playbook — 3-0.)* Reviewers want problem-solving, constraints, and the roads not taken — not a deliverable gallery.
- This is the heart of what gets you hired: **reasoning and decision-making path > polished final outcome.** *(IxDF — 3-0.)*

---

## 4. Outcomes & impact

- **Quantify against business/user impact:** engagement, conversion, retention, revenue saved, findability, user feedback. *"Numbers speak louder than words."* *(Kiley Rundle — 3-0.)* Numbers surfaced *early* are a primary skim signal.
- **No native business metrics? Benchmark.** Pick metrics representing key experience aspects and track **before/after** a design intervention. *(NN/g — primary source, 3-0.)*
  - Worked example: a **tree test** (Optimal Workshop's Treejack) took an IA findability score from **4.0/10 → 7.4/10 (+85%)** across the same 8 tasks. That's a concrete, defensible number with no revenue data.
  - Other credible proxies: usability task-completion / time-on-task, reduced error rates, decreased bounce, faster time-to-market, satisfaction (SUS/CSAT) deltas.
- **No numbers at all?** Impact can be **validated learnings** — what you proved, what shipped, what the team decided because of your work. State it clearly and honestly rather than faking precision.

> ⚠️ **Asterisks on famous numbers:** Simon Pan's Uber figures were obfuscated under NDA (illustrative, not audited). Don't invent or over-claim — reviewers smell it. Attribute team work honestly ("what *I* did").

---

## 5. Depth vs. brevity (scannability)

- Target a **~60–80% text / 20–40% media** balance, with outcomes surfaced up front. *(IxDF — 3-0, single-source benchmark.)*
- Keep read time **under ~5 minutes**; don't write 3,000+ words or show every iteration.
- Built for two passes: **headline + metric + section headers** carry the 90-second skim; the prose rewards the 5-minute read.

> ⚠️ **Refuted:** a "**tweet-sized / under-280-character**" cut rule and a "**20 minutes per case study**" budget both failed verification. Be concise, but concise ≠ tweet-length. Clarity and cause-and-effect beat artificial brevity.

---

## 6. Visuals

- **Annotate.** Bare mockups/screenshots with no annotation are the #1 visual pitfall flagged by a reviewer of 1,000+ portfolios (Alicja Suska). Tell the reader *what to look at and why*.
- Use **before/after** pairs to make impact legible at a glance — this is your single highest-value visual.
- Show **process artifacts** (sketches, flows, decision diagrams) only where they *carry an insight*, not as proof-of-effort wallpaper.
- Captions do narrative work: a one-line caption stating the decision turns a screenshot into evidence.
- **Common mistakes:** un-annotated screens; dumping every iteration; visual polish with no explanation; process artifacts that don't connect to a decision.

## 7. What gets a MID-LEVEL designer hired

- **Curate to ~3–4 strong, role-tailored case studies** — quality over quantity. (IxDF says 3–4; UX Design Institute "never more than five"; UXfolio 3–5 — **3-0**.) Dumping every career project is a common rejection driver. World Design Council: *"3 strong ones are more impactful."*
- **Tailor each study to the target role/domain** so reviewers see how you'd contribute to *their* team. (A Figma recruiter: align work to the target domain.)
- **Prove problem-solving + the ability to narrate it.** At mid-level the *designs themselves* still get scrutinized (unlike senior, where the portfolio mainly "gets you in the room" and the interview tests leadership/decision-making). So: solid craft **and** a clear thinking narrative.
- **Most common rejection reasons:** no tailoring (generic dump), shallow process (deliverables with no reasoning), no impact/metrics, weak role attribution on team projects, un-annotated visuals.

> **Open gap (be aware):** despite searching for it, no surviving claim isolated a crisp, *named* mid-vs-senior bar from Google/Airbnb/Stripe/Meta directly — this guidance comes from IxDF, individual design managers (Kiley Rundle, Morgane Peng, Alicja Suska), and recruiter-adjacent blogs. Treat the mid-level specifics as well-supported convention, not company policy.

## 8. Do's & Don'ts

**Do**
- Open with the bottom line (problem + solution + impact) before background.
- Title with subject + challenge + impact.
- Drive the story with research *insights*; mention methods in passing.
- Distill to ~3 key decisions/tradeoffs; show roads not taken.
- Quantify impact; benchmark (tree test / usability deltas) when no business metrics exist.
- Annotate every visual; use before/after.
- Curate 3–4 role-tailored studies; always end with reflection/lessons.

**Don't**
- Run the rote linear template as disconnected, clinical sections.
- Use research methods as section titles.
- Bury the value prop under setup.
- Show every iteration / chase 3,000+ words.
- Post un-annotated mockups or process-artifact wallpaper.
- Over-claim or fake metrics; mis-attribute team work.
- Pad the portfolio with every project you've ever touched.

## The reusable template (copy this skeleton)

```
1. HOOK / TITLE        Subject + challenge + impact. One line that creates curiosity.

2. BLUF SUMMARY        Problem · Solution · Impact (metrics) · Your role.
   (~90s grasp)         The whole story in 3–4 sentences + headline numbers.

3. CONTEXT             Scope, constraints, timeline, your specific role on the team.

4. STORY
   ├─ Insight          The 1–3 research findings that mattered (not the method list).
   ├─ Decisions        3 key decisions/tradeoffs — what you chose and what you rejected.
   ├─ Design           The solution, shown with annotated visuals + before/after.
   └─ Validation       How you knew it worked (test, ship, metric).

5. OUTCOMES            Quantified: business metric, OR benchmarked UX metric
                        (tree-test findability, task-success, error-rate delta).

6. REFLECTION          What you learned / would do differently. (Mandatory.)

Apply across the portfolio:
  • 3–4 role-tailored studies
  • ~60–80% text / 20–40% media
  • Results-first, scannable in 5 minutes, annotated visuals
```

## Pre-flight checklist

- [ ] Can a skimmer get problem + solution + impact in **90 seconds**?
- [ ] Does the title carry **subject + challenge + impact**?
- [ ] Are research **insights** leading — with methods subordinated?
- [ ] Are there **~3 clear decisions/tradeoffs** (incl. roads not taken)?
- [ ] Is impact **quantified** (business metric *or* benchmarked UX metric)?
- [ ] Is every visual **annotated**? Is there a **before/after**?
- [ ] Read time **under ~5 minutes**? ~60–80% text / 20–40% media?
- [ ] Is the study **tailored** to the target role/domain?
- [ ] Only **3–4** studies in the portfolio, each ending in **reflection**?
- [ ] Is **your specific role** clear on team projects (no over-claiming)?

## Source-quality caveats (read before treating any number as law)

- Only **NN/g** is a true primary source here. **IxDF** is a credible secondary education authority. Most storytelling/structure guidance is **practitioner blogs** (UX Playbook, Design Bootcamp, DesignerUp) — good for heuristics, **not empirical hiring data**.
- Prescriptive numbers (**60–80/20–40**, **5 minutes**, **90 seconds**, **3–4 studies**) are **conventions/benchmarks, not measured laws.**
- The template space is **genuinely contested** — refuted in verification: a fixed "3 success metrics" rule, a tweet-sized cut rule, a "20 min/study" sequence, and a rigid six-part structure. **Favor a curated narrative over any fixed sequence.**
- The brief asked for FAANG/Awwwards/Read.cv hiring-side specifics; **none of the surviving verified claims cite those companies/platforms directly.** The hiring perspective is from IxDF + individual managers + recruiter-adjacent blogs.
